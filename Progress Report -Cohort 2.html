1xCapitHumanitarian Aid Distribution & Financial InclusionDuring the accelerator the xCapit team focused on developing a prototype that demonstrates how humanitarian aid can be distributed transparently using blockchain infrastructure while still remaining accessible to communities with limited digital connectivity. The system was designed around a Low-Tech Wallet (LTW) concept where beneficiaries interact with the platform through SMS rather than traditional mobile wallets. Over the course of the cohort the team implemented the core transaction lifecycle: aid allocation by the humanitarian organization, beneficiary notification through SMS, merchant validation, and redemption of funds through a web-based merchant interface. By the end of the program the team had a working demonstration of how aid flows can be tracked on-chain while maintaining usability for populations without smartphones.The smart contract layer was implemented using custom Aiken validator scripts designed to enforce the conditions under which aid funds can be redeemed. Each aid allocation is locked in a UTxO that specifies both the beneficiary identifier and the authorized merchant network. The off-chain infrastructure handles SMS message verification and transaction construction before submitting the final transaction to the blockchain. API services coordinate interactions between the SMS gateway, the merchant dashboard, and the blockchain transaction layer. This hybrid architecture allows the blockchain to enforce the financial logic while operational workflows remain off-chain.The frontend environment was intentionally kept simple in order to reflect the realities of humanitarian aid distribution. Beneficiaries interact with the system primarily through SMS commands rather than mobile applications. Merchants use a lightweight web interface where they can verify beneficiary eligibility, confirm purchase transactions, and trigger redemption of aid allocations. An administrative dashboard allows aid organizations to allocate funds, track distribution activity, and monitor the performance of participating merchants. While the user interface remains minimal, it successfully demonstrates the full operational workflow of the system.From a blockchain perspective, the system models each aid allocation as a locked UTxO governed by a validator script. When a beneficiary confirms a purchase via SMS, the backend constructs a transaction that consumes the locked UTxO and transfers funds to the merchant’s address. This structure ensures that aid funds cannot be double spent and that each redemption event remains traceable on-chain. The use of discrete UTxOs also allows aid organizations to allocate funds to multiple beneficiaries simultaneously without requiring a centralized ledger.Because the system operates in a humanitarian context, particular attention was given to transparency and auditability of aid flows. At the same time the architecture avoids storing sensitive beneficiary data on-chain. Instead, identity references remain off-chain while the blockchain records only transaction-level information required for auditing. This design balances transparency with privacy and aligns with common humanitarian data protection practices.One of the main technical challenges encountered during development was the integration between SMS-based communication channels and blockchain transaction flows. Ensuring that beneficiary confirmation messages could securely trigger blockchain transactions required careful backend orchestration. Additionally, distributing aid to large numbers of beneficiaries created challenges around UTxO management and transaction batching.A key takeaway from the project is that blockchain infrastructure can support financial inclusion initiatives when paired with low-tech access layers. However, the complexity of bridging traditional communication channels with blockchain transactions should not be underestimated. Most of the system complexity ultimately resides in the off-chain coordination layer rather than in the smart contracts themselves.Future iterations of the platform should focus on improving scalability for large humanitarian deployments and exploring integration with stablecoins in order to reduce exposure to cryptocurrency price volatility. Additional work could also focus on improving beneficiary authentication mechanisms and exploring offline transaction confirmation models for areas with intermittent connectivity.Mentorship sessions focused primarily on reviewing validator logic and transaction flow design. Particular attention was given to ensuring that aid allocations cannot be double spent and that the redemption process remains secure even when thousands of allocations are issued simultaneously. Additional guidance was provided on UTxO management strategies to reduce transaction congestion during large distribution events.

The team worked on demonstrating how plastic recovery events recorded by recycling partners could be tokenized and made visible through a transparent ledger. The discussions throughout the program centered on interoperability with other impact-driven systems and how Plastiks’ tokenization model could potentially interact with complementary platforms focused on sustainability and environmental reporting.The Plastiks platform relies on smart contracts that manage the issuance and lifecycle of NFTs representing plastic recovery events. Each NFT corresponds to a verified recycling activity, with metadata describing the quantity of recovered plastic, the recycling partner involved, and the geographic origin of the activity. The minting logic is tied to validation workflows performed by recovery partners and platform administrators. Off-chain APIs coordinate the ingestion of recovery data, metadata generation, and NFT minting operations before submitting the transaction to the Cardano network. This architecture allows the blockchain layer to serve primarily as an integrity and traceability mechanism while operational processes remain off-chain.Plastiks already operates with a relatively mature user interface compared to many early-stage projects in the cohort. The platform includes dashboards used by recycling partners, sustainability teams, and corporate stakeholders interested in tracking plastic recovery commitments. These dashboards display recovery metrics, issued NFTs, and environmental impact indicators linked to recycling activities. During the accelerator the focus was less on building new frontend functionality and more on discussing how existing dashboards could support integrations with other sustainability platforms and reporting tools.From a blockchain perspective, the system represents each verified plastic recovery event as a tokenized asset recorded on the Cardano ledger. Ownership of these tokens reflects the traceability of the recycling event and provides an immutable record of the activity. The UTxO model works particularly well in this context because each recovery event can be represented as an individual asset tied to a specific transaction, making the traceability of environmental actions easier to audit.Plastiks operates in a regulatory environment increasingly influenced by global sustainability reporting frameworks and extended producer responsibility regulations. While the blockchain infrastructure itself is technology-agnostic with regard to regulation, the data recorded through the platform can support compliance reporting for organizations that must demonstrate measurable plastic recovery activities.One of the main challenges discussed during the cohort was the integration of real-world recycling supply chains with digital traceability systems. While blockchain infrastructure can ensure transparency once data is recorded, the reliability of the system ultimately depends on the accuracy of data reported by recycling operators and recovery partners.The project highlighted a recurring theme seen across several accelerator teams: blockchain systems work best when they serve as an integrity layer for real-world processes rather than attempting to replace those processes entirely. In Plastiks’ case, the value comes from anchoring verified recycling data to a transparent ledger rather than managing the recycling operations themselves.Future development opportunities discussed during the accelerator included expanding the network of recovery partners, integrating additional sustainability reporting tools, and exploring interoperability with other environmental platforms participating in the accelerator ecosystem.Mentorship interactions focused less on core protocol design and more on ecosystem strategy. Discussions centered around how the Plastiks platform could interoperate with other sustainability-focused projects and how blockchain traceability could complement existing environmental reporting workflows.

3TradeChainXCarbon Markets, MRV, and Tokenized Environmental AssetsTradeChainX focused on building a blockchain-based MRV (Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification) infrastructure designed to improve transparency and credibility in carbon markets. The core objective of the project was to create a system where carbon credit lifecycle events — from project registration to issuance and eventual retirement — could be recorded in a transparent and auditable manner. During the accelerator the team worked on defining a multi-stage architecture where project developers register carbon projects, verification bodies confirm environmental outcomes, and the resulting credits are issued as tokenized assets anchored on the Cardano blockchain. The PoC demonstrated how verification events could trigger on-chain asset creation, effectively linking real-world environmental actions with digital records.The contract architecture was designed around multiple validator scripts managing different stages of the carbon credit lifecycle. Instead of a single minting policy, the system uses a structured workflow where project registration, verification approval, and credit issuance are represented by separate transaction states. Custom Aiken smart contracts enforce transitions between these states, ensuring that credits cannot be issued before verification steps are completed. Off-chain services orchestrate the process by collecting verification data, validating inputs, and constructing transactions before submitting them to the blockchain. API endpoints connect the verification workflows, the operator interface, and the blockchain layer.The prototype includes an operational dashboard used primarily by project developers and platform administrators. Through the interface users can register carbon projects, upload verification documentation, and monitor the progress of verification stages before credits are issued. The dashboard also allows operators to track minted carbon credits and monitor retirement events once credits are consumed. While the frontend remains relatively simple at this stage, it demonstrates the end-to-end workflow from project registration through credit lifecycle management.From a blockchain perspective the system models the carbon credit lifecycle through state transitions encoded in the UTxO model. Each project begins as a registration transaction, which produces a UTxO representing the project state. Subsequent verification events consume the previous UTxO and produce a new one representing an updated verification stage. Once all required verification conditions are met, a minting transaction creates carbon credit tokens associated with the verified project. This approach allows the entire lifecycle of a carbon credit to be represented as a sequence of traceable transactions.Carbon markets are heavily influenced by international standards such as Verra and Gold Standard, which define verification methodologies for environmental impact. The TradeChainX architecture attempts to mirror these verification steps within the blockchain workflow. While the PoC does not replace existing certification bodies, it demonstrates how blockchain infrastructure could improve transparency and reduce the risk of double counting in carbon credit issuance.One of the major challenges encountered during development was modeling complex verification workflows within the relatively strict structure of the UTxO model. Carbon projects often involve multiple verification stages and external stakeholders, which required careful design of transaction states and validator conditions. Another challenge was ensuring that token issuance remains tied to verifiable environmental outcomes rather than arbitrary asset creation.A key insight from the project is that blockchain infrastructure can significantly improve transparency in carbon markets, but only when integrated with credible verification processes. The blockchain itself cannot determine whether environmental claims are valid; instead it provides a transparent record of verification decisions made by trusted actors.The next stage of development should focus on expanding the verification workflow and integrating external verification bodies directly into the platform. Additional work may also explore secondary marketplaces where issued carbon credits can be traded or retired by organizations seeking to offset emissions.Mentorship sessions focused heavily on architecture design and lifecycle modeling. Particular attention was given to ensuring that smart contracts enforce correct state transitions and prevent credits from being minted without proper verification steps. Discussions also explored how the platform could eventually support multiple verification frameworks without hardcoding them directly into the contract logic.

4Fuel SwitchRenewable Energy Certification & CBAM Compliance for Electricity ExportsFuel Switch focused on building a prototype system designed to issue and manage Renewable Energy Certificates (RECs) on Cardano in order to support transparent electricity export reporting for the Western Balkans. The problem being addressed is particularly relevant in the context of the European Union’s Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), which requires exporters to demonstrate the carbon intensity of electricity imports. The PoC demonstrates how verified renewable energy production data can be transformed into tokenized certificates whose lifecycle is recorded on-chain. During the accelerator the team implemented a working architecture that receives generation data from energy producers, validates the information through backend services, and mints NFT-based certificates representing discrete batches of renewable energy. The system also includes logic for certificate retirement once energy has been consumed or reported for compliance purposes.The smart contract layer is implemented as an Aiken-based minting policy compiled to Plutus V3, responsible for enforcing the rules governing certificate creation and retirement. The validator ensures that each certificate NFT follows a deterministic naming structure tied to the energy batch metadata (site name, production date, energy amount, and batch identifier). The contract also enforces strict limits on the maximum energy represented per token to prevent inflated certificate claims. On the off-chain side, the backend is implemented in Node.js with Express, using @lucid-evolution/lucid to construct and sign transactions. API endpoints process energy production data submitted by operators and orchestrate the minting process once validation checks are completed.The prototype includes a lightweight operator dashboard designed to simulate the workflow of an energy certificate registry. Through the interface operators can submit generation data, view issued certificates, and track certificate retirement events. The dashboard provides feedback on transaction status and includes links to blockchain explorers where the resulting transactions can be verified. While the interface remains relatively minimal, it demonstrates the core user journey of submitting production data and receiving verifiable certificates anchored on-chain.The blockchain interaction layer relies heavily on careful UTxO management strategies in order to support concurrent minting operations. The backend implements a fan-out strategy, where large ADA UTxOs are split into multiple smaller inputs that can be used for parallel transaction construction. This approach reduces the risk of transaction conflicts when multiple certificates are minted simultaneously. The system also enforces deterministic asset naming, ensuring that each certificate is uniquely tied to a specific production batch.The architecture was designed specifically with EU CBAM compliance in mind, where exporters must demonstrate that electricity imports originate from renewable sources. By anchoring production data to blockchain-based certificates, the system provides a transparent audit trail for energy generation events. While the PoC does not replace official certification bodies, it demonstrates how blockchain infrastructure could support future digital reporting mechanisms for cross-border energy trade.One of the main technical challenges encountered during development was ensuring that certificate minting remains strictly tied to validated production data. Because the blockchain cannot independently verify energy generation, the backend validation layer plays a critical role in preventing incorrect data from being tokenized. Another challenge involved managing UTxO concurrency when multiple minting transactions were executed in parallel.The project highlighted the importance of combining on-chain validation with robust off-chain verification mechanisms. While the blockchain can enforce deterministic asset creation rules, ensuring the integrity of energy production data ultimately depends on the systems supplying that data.Future work will focus on integrating automated data feeds from energy generation systems and exploring the possibility of switching from NFT-based certificates to fungible token models representing aggregated energy production. Additional work may also include integration with national energy registries or reporting systems used by regulators.Mentorship sessions focused extensively on validator logic, transaction construction strategies, and UTxO management. Particular attention was given to ensuring that the minting policy cannot be exploited to generate certificates without valid metadata and that concurrency issues are minimized when issuing certificates at scale.

5SALADigital Identity & Credential Verification for Academic InstitutionsThe SALA team focused on building a prototype system that allows educational institutions to issue digitally verifiable academic credentials anchored on the Cardano blockchain. The initiative was developed in collaboration with the University of Sarajevo and aims to address a common challenge faced by universities and employers: verifying the authenticity of academic certificates and preventing credential fraud. During the accelerator the team implemented a working proof of concept where academic certificates are issued digitally and their integrity is secured through blockchain anchoring. The system allows third parties, such as employers or partner institutions, to verify the authenticity of a certificate by scanning a QR code linked to the blockchain record. The PoC demonstrated how credential documents stored off-chain can be linked to immutable on-chain hashes that guarantee their integrity.The prototype uses a combination of smart contract interactions and off-chain services to anchor credential hashes to the blockchain. When a certificate is issued, a cryptographic hash of the document is generated and included in a transaction that records the hash on-chain. This approach ensures that the document cannot be altered without invalidating the associated blockchain record. The backend services handle document ingestion, hash generation, and transaction submission, while the blockchain acts as a permanent integrity layer. While the initial prototype relied on NFT-style credential representations, discussions during the accelerator highlighted the potential benefits of moving toward W3C Verifiable Credentials (VCs) for future iterations of the system.The project includes a sandbox environment where users can simulate the process of issuing and verifying credentials. Through the interface, administrators can upload certificate documents and issue blockchain-anchored verification records. External users can then scan a QR code or upload a certificate file to verify whether the document matches the hash stored on-chain. The user interface is intentionally straightforward and designed to demonstrate the verification process rather than to serve as a fully operational credential management system.The blockchain component of the system functions primarily as an integrity anchor rather than a data storage layer. Certificate documents remain stored off-chain, while the blockchain records only their cryptographic hashes. This approach avoids unnecessary on-chain data storage while still allowing third parties to independently verify document authenticity. Each verification record is tied to a specific transaction, providing an immutable timestamp for when the credential was issued.The architecture aligns well with emerging digital identity and credential verification frameworks that prioritize privacy and data ownership. By storing only document hashes on-chain, the system avoids exposing sensitive personal information while still allowing verification of authenticity. This approach is compatible with broader trends in digital identity systems that separate identity data from verification mechanisms.One of the main architectural discussions during the cohort centered around the choice of representing credentials as NFTs. While NFTs can technically represent credentials, they may not always align with the evolving standards used in digital identity ecosystems. The team therefore explored how the architecture could eventually support Verifiable Credentials and decentralized identity frameworks while still leveraging blockchain anchoring for trust and transparency.The project highlighted how blockchain infrastructure can strengthen trust in academic credentials by providing a tamper-resistant verification layer. However, it also demonstrated that blockchain alone does not solve identity management challenges and must be integrated with broader digital identity standards.Future work should focus on transitioning from an NFT-based credential representation toward W3C Verifiable Credential standards. This would allow the platform to interoperate with emerging decentralized identity ecosystems while maintaining the blockchain anchoring mechanism for integrity verification.Mentorship sessions focused on architecture review and long-term design considerations. In particular, discussions centered on how the system could evolve beyond a proof-of-concept toward a standards-compliant credential infrastructure that universities could realistically adopt. The collaboration with the University of Sarajevo also provided valuable insights into how academic institutions might integrate such systems into existing administrative workflows.

The goal of the initiative is to help producers, aggregators, and buyers verify the origin and sustainability of agricultural products. During the accelerator the team developed a prototype capable of representing agricultural production batches as tokenized assets whose lifecycle can be tracked across multiple supply chain stages. The system allows events such as harvesting, aggregation, transport, and delivery to be recorded and linked to a specific batch identifier, creating a traceable digital record of the product’s journey.The smart contract layer relies on custom Aiken validators responsible for minting and managing supply-chain tokens representing agricultural batches. Each token corresponds to a specific production batch and includes metadata describing the product type, geographic origin, and production date. Off-chain APIs manage the ingestion of supply chain data from producers and logistics operators before constructing transactions that update the blockchain record. The architecture intentionally keeps most operational logic off-chain while using the blockchain primarily as a transparency and verification layer.The prototype includes a dashboard designed for supply chain operators rather than end consumers. Through the interface, users can register new agricultural batches, record supply chain events, and view the traceability history associated with each batch token. The dashboard also provides visual summaries of product origin, logistics stages, and verification events. While the frontend remains relatively simple, it successfully demonstrates how supply chain actors can interact with the traceability system without needing technical blockchain knowledge.From a blockchain perspective, each agricultural batch is represented as a token minted through a transaction that anchors the initial production event. Subsequent supply chain updates are recorded through additional transactions that reference the batch identifier. The use of the UTxO model allows each batch to maintain a clear lineage of transactions, effectively creating an auditable trail that documents the movement of goods through the supply chain.Agricultural exports increasingly require traceability mechanisms in order to comply with sustainability reporting and import regulations in international markets. The Green Giraffe architecture supports these requirements by allowing producers and exporters to demonstrate the provenance and movement of agricultural goods. Although the PoC does not integrate directly with regulatory systems, it demonstrates how blockchain infrastructure could support certification and reporting workflows.One of the primary challenges discussed during the cohort was ensuring the reliability of field-level data. Collecting reliable production and logistics data from multiple actors across the supply chain therefore remains a significant operational challenge.The project highlighted a recurring theme seen across many supply chain blockchain initiatives: the technology works well for traceability once the data is available, but the hardest part is ensuring that the initial data input is trustworthy. In many cases, this requires partnerships with trusted aggregators or certification bodies.Future development should explore integration with IoT devices, digital logistics systems, or certification bodies capable of providing trusted data feeds. These integrations could significantly improve the reliability of supply chain data and strengthen the credibility of the traceability records.Mentorship sessions focused on modeling supply chain events in a way that maps cleanly to blockchain transactions. Discussions also explored how the tokenization model could scale to large agricultural networks where thousands of production batches may be tracked simultaneously.

7SolarVillageEnergy Settlement & Carbon Accounting for Rural MicrogridsSolarVillage focused on developing a decentralized energy settlement platform designed for rural and peri-urban microgrid environments. The project addresses several interconnected challenges: reliable energy payment systems in regions with limited banking infrastructure, transparent accounting for electricity delivery, and credible carbon credit generation linked to clean energy production. During the accelerator the team implemented a prototype architecture where day-to-day energy transactions are settled through Cardano Hydra, allowing high-frequency and low-cost accounting operations, while carbon asset issuance and certification events are anchored on Cardano Layer 1. The prototype successfully demonstrated Hydra head initialization, token commitment into the head, transaction execution within the Hydra environment, and the eventual decommitment and settlement of balances back to the main chain.The contract layer was implemented primarily using Aiken, both for Hydra-side validation logic and Layer 1 asset issuance policies. Off-chain orchestration services coordinate the interaction between microgrid operators, the Hydra runtime environment, and the Cardano blockchain. The system uses a combination of Hydra nodes, etcd coordination services, and backend scripts to manage the lifecycle of Hydra heads and the associated transactions. The architecture also integrates Blockfrost APIs for blockchain queries and transaction submission where necessary.The prototype includes a basic web interface used primarily for operational monitoring and testing of the system. Through this interface operators can initialize Hydra heads, mint internal accounting tokens, and simulate energy settlement flows. The frontend is not intended to represent a final user interface but rather to demonstrate the core operational logic of the platform. In a production environment this interface would likely be integrated into existing microgrid management systems used by energy operators.Within the Hydra environment, energy transactions are represented through internal accounting tokens corresponding to delivered electricity (e.g., Wh units). UTxOs representing these tokens are committed into the Hydra head, where multiple settlement transactions can occur rapidly without interacting directly with the main Cardano chain. Periodically, the system anchors the resulting state back to Layer 1, ensuring that the overall accounting remains verifiable and tamper-resistant.The system architecture anticipates alignment with emerging carbon market frameworks and renewable energy reporting standards. By linking energy delivery data to blockchain records, the platform provides a transparent audit trail that could support carbon credit issuance based on verified clean energy generation. The architecture was also designed with the expectation that future regulatory environments may require verifiable reporting of renewable energy production.One of the most technically demanding aspects of the project was the operational complexity of running a Hydra-based infrastructure environment. Setting up Hydra nodes, synchronizing Cardano nodes, managing state persistence, and coordinating off-chain transaction flows required significant infrastructure work. Another challenge involved designing a token accounting model that accurately represents real-world energy delivery without introducing inconsistencies in the settlement process.The project demonstrated that Hydra can be a strong fit for high-frequency settlement environments such as microgrid energy markets. However, the development process also highlighted that running Hydra infrastructure currently requires considerable operational expertise, especially when multiple nodes and off-chain services must interact reliably.Future development should focus on automating the Hydra lifecycle processes, including head initialization, commit/decommit flows, and monitoring of settlement events. Additional work could also explore integrating the platform with real energy metering systems in order to automate the ingestion of generation data and reduce manual data entry.Mentorship discussions focused heavily on architecture design and infrastructure strategy. Particular attention was given to evaluating whether Hydra was the appropriate scaling solution for microgrid settlement and how the system could evolve toward a production-ready deployment once the operational complexity of the infrastructure is better managed.

The goal of the project is to create a mechanism where environmental initiatives—such as reforestation programs, conservation efforts, or sustainability projects—can be represented through verifiable digital tokens tied to measurable environmental outcomes. During the accelerator the team focused on building the architecture required to register projects, verify impact events, and issue tokens that represent the verified impact associated with those initiatives. The prototype demonstrated how environmental data from project operators could be processed through verification steps before triggering asset issuance on the Cardano blockchain.The smart contract logic for ClimaFi focuses on controlled token minting tied to verified project events. Instead of allowing arbitrary token creation, the contract layer enforces that tokens can only be minted once verification conditions are met. These verification signals are processed through backend services that validate incoming project data before constructing blockchain transactions. The system integrates APIs that coordinate project registration, verification workflows, and token minting operations. In this architecture the blockchain acts as the final verification layer where tokens representing environmental impact are issued and recorded.The prototype includes a web-based dashboard designed for project operators and platform administrators. Through the interface, users can register new environmental initiatives, submit supporting documentation or data related to project activities, and monitor verification status before tokens are issued. The dashboard also provides visibility into issued tokens and the associated environmental metrics, allowing stakeholders to track how much impact has been generated by different projects.From a blockchain perspective, each environmental impact token represents a discrete verified event tied to a specific project. The minting transaction records metadata references describing the project and the type of environmental outcome achieved. By anchoring this information on-chain, the system creates an immutable record linking environmental claims to blockchain transactions. This helps reduce the risk of duplicate reporting or unverifiable environmental claims in climate finance initiatives.Climate finance markets rely heavily on verification frameworks such as MRV (Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification) methodologies used in carbon markets. While the ClimaFi prototype does not replace existing verification bodies, the system was designed to complement these frameworks by providing a transparent ledger where verification outcomes can be recorded. This approach could potentially strengthen trust in climate finance programs by making verification events auditable.One of the main challenges encountered during development was ensuring that token issuance remains tightly coupled to verified environmental outcomes. Because blockchain systems cannot independently validate environmental data, the platform must rely on robust off-chain verification mechanisms before minting tokens. Designing this verification pipeline required careful coordination between data ingestion systems and blockchain transaction logic.The project reinforced the idea that blockchain infrastructure works best as an integrity and transparency layer rather than a standalone verification system. While blockchain can record environmental claims in a tamper-resistant way, credible verification processes must exist outside the chain to validate those claims.Future development should focus on integrating the platform with established verification providers and expanding the types of environmental initiatives that can be represented through the system. Additional work could also explore secondary markets where verified impact tokens can be traded or retired by organizations seeking to demonstrate sustainability commitments.Mentorship sessions focused on helping the team structure their verification workflow in a way that maps cleanly to blockchain transaction events. Discussions also explored how the tokenization model could evolve to support multiple environmental asset types while maintaining strong verification guarantees.
